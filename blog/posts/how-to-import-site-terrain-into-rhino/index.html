How to Import Site Terrain into Rhino (The Easy Way)
25 June 2026 · 2 min · Aki

So you’re trying to get real site terrain into Rhino. Good news: it’s not complicated once you know the two routes. Let me walk you through both.

The problem most people hit first

You’ve got a site. You need topography. Rhino doesn’t pull elevation data from anywhere natively, so you’re either tracing contours off a PDF survey or wrestling with mesh files that don’t match your actual site. Neither is a good use of your time.

Route 1: XYZ point cloud to mesh

Export an XYZ CSV from Topo-grapher, draw your site boundary on the map, and download. You get a grid of elevation points covering your exact site. In Rhino, go to File > Import, select your CSV, and Rhino brings it in as a point cloud. From there, run Patch or Drape to build a surface, or use QuadRemesh if you want a clean mesh for rendering. If you’re in Grasshopper, even simpler: feed the XYZ points into a Delaunay mesh component and you’ve got a structured terrain surface in about 10 nodes.

Route 2: DXF contours

Topo-grapher also exports DXF contour lines at adaptive intervals based on your site’s relief. Import the DXF directly into Rhino, then loft or patch between the contours to build your surface. Good option if you prefer working from lines rather than points.

Which one to use

Point cloud if you want speed and you’re heading into Grasshopper. DXF if you want contour lines you can adjust before building the surface. Either way, you’re starting from real government LiDAR data, not a traced PDF. Go draw your site boundary at topo-grapher.com and download your file. Takes about 30 seconds.
