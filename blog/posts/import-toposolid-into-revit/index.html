How to Import a Toposolid into Revit (Without the Headache)

Bring real site terrain into Revit as a native toposolid you can actually edit, not a locked IFC. Here's how, and why it saves hours per project.
21 June 2026 · 2 min · Aki

If you’ve ever tried to build accurate terrain in Revit, SketchUp or Rhino by hand, you already know the feeling. It’s slow, it’s fiddly, and somehow the ground never quite sits where the survey says it should.

Here in Iceland it’s a bit of a ritual. For most sites you can dig up a survey document, on paper, with the elevation points marked. Then you get to redraw the whole site and its levels by hand, square by square, hoping the result is close enough to reality to design against. Spoiler: it usually isn’t, not as close as you’d actually want.

So I built a tool to do the boring part for me. It pulls real elevations from local and national surveys and builds the site for you, right inside the software you already use.

The toposolid bit is the hard bit#

Plenty of tools can spit out terrain. The part nobody seems to get right is handing you a toposolid you can actually work with natively in Revit, not a frozen IFC mesh you have to fight every time the design moves.

That’s the difference with topo-grapher. You can import your terrain as a real toposolid instead of an IFC, which means you can adjust the landscape as the project changes, push it to the elevation you need, and have it behave properly inside your normal workflow. No re-linking, no “why won’t this surface edit,” no quietly rebuilding it by hand anyway.

One honest caveat#

I’ll always tell you to check the result against your surveyor or whatever site survey you already have. It’s good data, but it’s your project and your liability, so trust, but verify.

That said, this saves a genuinely silly number of hours on every project. Hours you can spend designing instead of tracing contour lines, plus the money it quietly saves the whole team.

Give it a go at topo-grapher.com, and ask away if you have any questions.
