DTM vs DSM: what's the difference (and which do you need)?

A DTM (Digital Terrain Model) represents the bare ground with buildings and vegetation stripped away. A DSM (Digital Surface Model) represents the top of everything on the ground — roofs, tree canopy, structures. For site topography in BIM you almost always want the DTM.

By Aki, founder of Topo-grapher · Updated June 2026

Both come from the same kind of source data, usually airborne LiDAR, but they answer different questions. The DTM asks "where is the ground?"; the DSM asks "what is the highest thing at this point?" Choosing the wrong one is one of the most common reasons a site model comes in looking lumpy or several metres too high.

Which do you need?

For nearly all architectural site work — placing a building, modelling cut and fill, showing context — you want the DTM, because you need the real ground surface. Reach for a DSM only when the objects on the site are the point: assessing the height of neighbouring buildings, tree screening, or rights-to-light studies.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a DTM and a DSM?

A DTM represents the bare ground with buildings and vegetation removed. A DSM represents the top of everything on the ground, including building roofs and tree canopy. The DSM is higher wherever objects sit on the terrain.

Should I use a DTM or DSM for site topography in BIM?

Use a DTM. For site context, grading, and a terrain surface under your building, you want the bare ground. A DSM is useful when you specifically need the heights of existing buildings or tree cover.

Does Topo-grapher use a DTM or a DSM?

It uses the best terrain source per country and labels whether that source is a DTM or DSM. When buildings are added on a DSM source, their base is set to the surface height minus building height so they sit correctly on the ground.
